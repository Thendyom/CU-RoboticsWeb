  \section*{Abstract}

  \begin{guidance}
  Write the abstract last. It should stand on its own and answer five questions:
  What problem did you study? Why does it matter? What did you do? What did you
  find? What is the main limitation? Use exact, bounded claims. Avoid turning
  the abstract into a list of chapters or introducing terms that are not
  explained within the abstract.

  A useful structure is one short motivation paragraph, one method paragraph,
  and one results-and-meaning paragraph. Target roughly 150--250 words unless
  your program specifies otherwise.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  A clear abstract became much easier to write after separating the main
  investigation from optional exploratory work. When two parts answer different
  questions or rely on different levels of evidence, they should not be
  presented as one equally supported result.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \textit{Example opening:}
  Autonomous and intelligent systems must perform reliably under practical
  constraints. This thesis investigates whether a proposed robotic subsystem
  can satisfy a defined task objective while remaining sufficiently robust and
  resource-efficient for its intended operating context.

  \section{Introduction}

  \begin{guidance}
  Introduce the practical problem before the implementation. A reader should
  understand the system context, stakeholders, intended behavior, and exact
  thesis goal before encountering algorithms, hardware, or architecture
  details. End the introduction with explicit research questions, the scope,
  and a concise list of contributions.

  Define the goal early and make it testable. State what the system should do,
  under which conditions, and how success will be judged. Also state what the
  thesis does not attempt to solve. Clear boundaries make later design and
  evaluation decisions easier to justify.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  The largest improvement to the overall structure came from writing the goal
  as one precise sentence before expanding the technical chapters. Whenever a
  section did not help define, implement, or evaluate that goal, it was either
  shortened, moved, or removed.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \subsection{Example Research Questions}

  \begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}]
    \item Can the proposed system perform the defined task under the stated
      operating conditions?
    \item Which design choices have the greatest effect on task performance,
      robustness, and resource use?
    \item Where does the system fail, and what do those failures imply for
      practical use?
  \end{enumerate}

  \subsection{Example Scope Statement}

  This thesis covers the design, implementation, and controlled evaluation of
  one robotic or intelligent subsystem. Integration assumptions and relevant
  interfaces are documented, but full product development, certification, and
  unrestricted real-world deployment are outside the scope.

  \section{Statement and Motivation of Research}

  \begin{guidance}
  Explain why the research question is worth answering and position it against
  the existing literature. Organize related work by ideas or assumptions, not
  as one paragraph per paper. State what remains unknown after the literature
  review and show how your experiment addresses that gap.

  Keep motivation, related work, and thesis positioning distinct:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Motivation:} why the problem matters in its system context;
    \item \textbf{Related work:} what existing approaches already establish;
    \item \textbf{Positioning:} the specific unresolved question your thesis tests.
  \end{itemize}
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  The literature review became more useful when each group of sources led to a
  design decision, hypothesis, or evaluation criterion. Sources that did not
  affect the investigation were summarized briefly instead of being given equal
  space.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \textit{Example positioning statement:}
  Existing work demonstrates several approaches to this class of robotic
  problem. However, their trade-offs under the constraints of the intended
  operating context remain unclear. This thesis therefore compares selected
  design choices under a shared evaluation protocol.

  \section{Description of the Investigation}

  \begin{guidance}
  This is the technical core. Describe the system and investigation so another
  researcher could reproduce the work and understand why each design decision
  was made. Include:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item requirements, assumptions, and success criteria;
    \item the system architecture and interfaces between components;
    \item hardware, software, data, and operating conditions;
    \item implementation decisions and relevant alternatives;
    \item the experimental protocol, baselines, and controlled variants;
    \item evaluation metrics, repeated trials, and logging procedures.
  \end{itemize}

  When comparing design choices, change as few factors as possible at once.
  Keep an explicit experiment map showing what remains fixed. Store exact
  configurations, source code, calibration values, and evaluation logs outside
  the document so that the written account can be checked against the work that
  was actually executed.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  Comparisons were easiest to explain when the baseline and all fixed conditions
  were documented before new variants were tested. If multiple components,
  parameters, and operating conditions change together, the reason for an
  improvement or failure remains ambiguous.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \subsection{Example Reproducibility Checklist}

  \begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
requirements and interface definitions
hardware setup, calibration, and operating conditions
data sources, selection rules, and test scenarios
software configuration and random seeds
software and hardware versions
baseline definition and comparison protocol
metric implementation and success thresholds
saved logs and outputs for headline results
  \end{lstlisting}

  \section{Evaluation of the Investigation}

  \begin{guidance}
  Present results in the same order as the research questions and experiment
  sequence. Lead with the claim supported by each table or figure, then explain
  the evidence and its limits. Do not report only the best model.

  Select metrics that match the intended use. Depending on the project, these
  may include task success, estimation error, latency, energy use, robustness,
  safety-related events, or human interaction quality. Report variation across
  repeated trials and relevant operating conditions, not only a single average.

  Distinguish a measured result from an inferred explanation. Measurements can
  demonstrate what happened under the test protocol; additional evidence is
  needed to establish why it happened or whether it generalizes.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  A result table became much more informative after adding the intended role of
  each system variant and the conditions under which it was tested. The best
  average score was not automatically the best practical choice when resource
  use, consistency, or failure severity were also important.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \section{Discussion and Limitations}

  \begin{guidance}
  Discussion is not a second results section. Explain what the results mean,
  which claims are strongly supported, what alternative explanations remain,
  and where the evidence does not generalize. Connect each limitation to a
  concrete consequence for interpretation or future validation.

  Useful categories include assumptions, test-environment coverage, data or
  scenario bias, measurement quality, statistical stability, hardware and
  software constraints, safety, human factors, and mismatch between the
  research prototype and the intended real system.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  Limitations were strongest when each one stated its consequence. For example,
  testing only in a controlled environment does not merely reduce variety; it
  means the evidence cannot support unrestricted real-world reliability claims.
  Clear limitations improve the credibility of the supported conclusions.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \section{Conclusion and Future Work}

  \begin{guidance}
  Answer the research questions directly. State the strongest supported result,
  the practical recommendation, and the main boundary of the evidence. Future
  work should follow from missing evidence identified in the limitations, not
  from a generic list of possible extensions.

  A useful final paragraph states what you would do first if the project
  continued tomorrow.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  A useful conclusion was easier to write by answering each research question in
  one or two direct sentences before creating the surrounding narrative. Future
  work then followed naturally from the most important missing evidence rather
  than from a generic wish list.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \textit{Example final answer:}
  Under the stated test conditions, the proposed system satisfies the primary
  task objective and improves on the defined baseline. The selected design is
  therefore a suitable basis for further investigation. This conclusion remains
  limited by the evaluated scenarios and requires broader system-level
  validation before practical deployment claims are justified.

  \section{What Belongs in the Appendix}

  \begin{guidance}
  Use the appendix for material needed to audit or reproduce the thesis without
  interrupting the main argument. Examples include full result tables,
  additional trials, parameter tables, interface details, calibration
  procedures, questionnaires, safety analyses, and implementation notes. Refer
  to every useful appendix item from the main text.
  \end{guidance}

  \begin{experiencenote}
  Moving large supporting tables and detailed configurations to the appendix
  made the main argument easier to follow while preserving the information
  needed to audit and reproduce the work.
  \end{experiencenote}

  \section{Final Submission Checklist}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item Every research question is answered in the conclusion.
    \item Every headline claim points to a table, figure, or cited source.
    \item Main and exploratory evidence are clearly separated.
    \item Requirements, assumptions, interfaces, and success criteria are explicit.
    \item Test conditions, configurations, metrics, and repeated trials are documented.
    \item Limitations qualify the claims rather than appearing as disclaimers.
    \item Figures and tables are readable without relying on the surrounding text.
    \item Bibliography entries compile and all citations are used correctly.
    \item The final PDF is checked page by page after compilation.
  \end{itemize}
